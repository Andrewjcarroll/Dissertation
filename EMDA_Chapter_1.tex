\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
The increasing sensitivity of gravitational-wave detectors has enabled progressively more stringent tests of General Relativity (GR) \cite{Meidam2014,Berti2015,Yang2017}. Recent advances in gravitational-wave astronomy have led to the detection of signals from compact binary mergers, granting direct access to strongly gravitating, nonlinear regimes. All observed mergers to date are consistent with the predictions of GR \cite{Abbott2016,Abbott2016b,Abbott2019}.

Despite this agreement, the long-standing tension between GR and quantum mechanics remains unresolved. The broader field of quantum gravity seeks to address this discrepancy, motivating the exploration of alternative theories of gravity. Examples include Horndeski gravity \cite{Kobayashi2019_HorndeskiReview}, Lovelock gravity \cite{Padmanabhan2013_Lovelock,Lovelock1971_EinsteinTensor}, scalar Gauss–Bonnet gravity \cite{Mignemi1993_ChargedStringBH, Elley2022,Silva2021DynamicalDescalarization}, and Einstein–Maxwell–Dilaton–Axion (EMDA) theory \cite{Sen1992,Sen2008}.

In this work, we focus on the EMDA framework, examining the stability of its black-hole solutions as a prerequisite for producing inspiral waveforms.

As detector sensitivity improves and additional signals are recorded, increasingly precise tests of GR will become possible \cite{Yunes2013_LRR_GRTests,Yagi2016_BHTestsGR}. Waveforms predicted by alternative theories play a dual role: they guide searches for new signatures and provide benchmarks for testing those theories. Such comparisons may ultimately reveal measurable deviations from GR. Alternative theories generally lack the extensive waveform catalogs available in GR; current efforts often rely on phenomenological approaches, including post-Newtonian expansions and numerical-relativity simulations \cite{Yunes2009,Agathos2014}.

In standard GR, black holes are “bald”—that is, they are completely described by their mass, spin, and charge \cite{Israel1967,Carter1971}. By contrast, several extensions of GR predict scalarized black holes. For instance, recent studies have examined scalarization during mergers in scalar Gauss–Bonnet gravity \cite{Elley2022,Silva2021DynamicalDescalarization}. The EMDA theory, which arises in the low-energy limit of heterotic string theory, introduces both a scalar dilaton and an axion while retaining a well-posed initial-data formulation. The existence of scalar hair in EMDA depends on charge and spin. In the limit of vanishing axion coupling, EMDA reduces to the simpler Einstein–Maxwell–Dilaton (EMD) theory.


Although numerical simulations of binary black holes in the EMDA theory remain limited, progress has been made in the Einstein–Maxwell–Dilaton setting \cite{Julie2017,Hirschmann2018}, establishing a foundation for extending such studies to the full EMDA framework. It is not immediately clear whether the scalar hair predicted by the EMDA theory remains stable or eventually radiates away, leaving behind a remnant black hole characterized solely by its mass, spin, and charge. Previous studies have explored the related phenomena of scalarization and descalarization in other modified gravity theories \cite{Sotiriou2012,Cardoso2013,Sotiriou2014}.EMDA is a particularly compelling theory as it allows for direct tests of low energy string theory. Establishing the stability of Kerr–Sen black holes therefore represents an important direction for future investigation.

Previous work has explored astrophysical observations for potential evidence of Kerr–Sen black holes. Analyses of Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) shadow data suggest that Sgr A* favors the Kerr–Sen solution, while M87* favors Kerr but remains consistent with Kerr–Sen within current observational uncertainties \cite{Sahoo2024PRD, Sahoo2025}.

In this study, we extend these investigations by simulating head-on mergers governed by the full EMDA equations. We analyze the resulting waveforms and examine the stability of the remnants, thereby gaining insight into the persistence of scalar hair within the theory. Specifically, we evolve several head-on collisions of black holes in EMDA to determine the conditions under which the dilaton and axion fields survive the merger. An additional key question is to determine under what conditions initially unscalarized black holes will undergo scalarization.

\section{Einstein--Maxwell--Dilaton--Axion}
\label{sec:emda}
EMDA arises from low-energy string theory. This includes a U(1) gauge field and a scalar field that couples to the gauge field. In particular the action for low-energy heterotic string theory is::
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
S=\int \mathrm{d}^4 x\,\sqrt{-g}\Big[
&\frac{R}{8\pi G}-2(\nabla\phi)^2-e^{-2\alpha_0\phi}F^2\\
&-\tfrac12 e^{4\alpha_1\phi}(\nabla\kappa)^2-\kappa\,F_{ab}({}^*F)^{ab}
\Big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here, $g$ denotes the determinant of the metric, $R$ the Ricci scalar, $\phi$ the dilaton, $F_{ab}$ the Faraday tensor, and $\kappa$ the axion. In the absence of charge, the equations reduce to the standard Einstein equations. In contrast to the no-hair theorem, the EMDA theory admits two additional fields—the dilaton $\phi$ and the axion $\kappa$—which couple to the electromagnetic field and together constitute the “hair.” The parameters $\alpha_0$ and $\alpha_1$ set the coupling strengths; in this work we take $\alpha_0=\alpha_1=1$, corresponding to the low-energy limit of heterotic string theory. Setting $\alpha_0=\alpha_1=0$ reduces the theory to Einstein–Maxwell with minimally coupled scalar and axion fields.


To perform numerical evolutions of Kerr--Sen black holes, we formulate the equations as a Cauchy problem and use the BSSN formulation\cite{Shibata1995_BSSN, Baumgarte1999_BSSN, Baumgarte2010} of the Einstein equations, supplemented with the necessary terms to account for  electromagnetic fields, axion and dilaton. We also employ the puncture gauge condition\cite{Campanelli2006,Baker2006}. Initial data are prepared for head--on black hole collisions. The full equations of motion and the $3+1$ decomposition are presented in Appendix~\ref{sec:appx:eom}.

\section{Numerical methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Code Description} 
For these simulations, we modified the existing numerical-relativity framework \texttt{Dendro-GR}. \texttt{Dendro-GR} is a portable code that combines an octree-based adaptive multiresolution framework with automatic code generation for solving the EMDA equations. Spatial derivatives are computed using sixth-order finite-difference stencils, and time integration is performed with a fourth-order Runge--Kutta scheme. More details on \texttt{Dendro-GR} can be found in \cite{Fernando2019,Fernando2019b,Fernando2023,Black2025_ORBIT}.

\subsection{Initial Data}
Valid initial data must satisfy the constraint equations; in particular, we must satisfy the Hamiltonian, momentum, and Maxwell constraints. In natural units, these are given by
\begin{equation}
R + K^2 - K_{ij}K^{ij} = 16 \pi \rho,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
D_j(K^{ij} - \gamma^{ij}K) = 8 \pi J^i,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}$ is the three-dimensional Ricci scalar associated with the metric, $K_{ij}$ is the extrinsic curvature, $K$ is the trace of the extrinsic curvature, $\rho$ is the energy density, and $J^i$ is the current density.
\begin{equation}
D_i E^i = 0,
\qquad
D_i B^i = 0,
\end{equation}
The energy and current densities are defined as
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\rho \equiv& \Pi^2 + (D\phi)^2
+ e^{-2 \alpha_0 \phi}\big( E_a  E^a + B_a B^a\big) \\
&+ \tfrac{1}{4} e^{4 \alpha_1 \phi}\big(\Xi^2 + (D\kappa)^2\big),
\end{split}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
J^i \equiv 2 \Pi D^i \phi
+ 2 e^{-2 \alpha_0 \phi} \epsilon^{ijk} E_j B_k
+ \tfrac{1}{2} e^{4 \alpha_1 \phi} \Xi D^i \kappa.
\end{equation}
The EMDA theory possesses a mathematical structure that admits well-posed initial data. The formulation of such data has not been extensively investigated in the literature, although there have been some approaches to generating initial data for charged black holes \cite{Alcubierre2009,Bowen1985_ChargedInitialData,Mukherjee2022_ConformallyCurvedChargedSpinning,Zilhao2014,Zilhao2012}. In this work, we adapt techniques originally developed for constructing the initial data of charged black holes. Specifically, we employ an elliptic solver based on a modified version of the two-charged-punctures solver introduced in \cite{Bozzola2019}, augmented to incorporate contributions from the dilaton. A more detailed discussion of the initial-data construction is provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:appx:id}.

To analyze the numerical data, we compute the Newman--Penrose scalar $\Psi_4$, as well as the radiated electromagnetic quantities, extracting their multipole moments at $r \approx 100M$. Further details on the Waveform extraction are in Appendix C.

\subsection{Initially Unscalarized Black Holes}

To further assess the stability of scalar hair, we conducted a series of tests initialized with a Gaussian distribution of axion and dilaton fields centered at the origin, with amplitudes ranging from $10^{-1}$ to $10^{-7}$. These initial perturbations propagate outward, enabling us to examine whether initially unscalarized black holes can acquire and sustain scalar fields. Our results confirm that they do: a Kerr–Newman black hole in the EMDA theory rapidly scalarizes, developing both dilaton and axion hair. 

Remarkably, even in the absence of any initial scalar fields in the background, the system evolves to generate nontrivial scalar structure. This demonstrates that scalarization occurs dynamically and does not require preexisting scalar hair. The conserved quantities, including the total mass and charge, remain effectively constant throughout the evolution. Variations in the conserved quantities over the course of the evolution in both mass and charge are below \(0.2\,\%\). This indicates that our quantities remain stable and  conserved overtime. \section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We have simulated a range of different parameters for the head-on collisions of Kerr--Sen black holes. We have primarily been concerned with the persistence of hair.  
In summary, our simulations show that the scalar fields do not radiate away entirely, so long as there is a nonzero charge (and spin for the axion). We have found that Kerr--Sen black holes are stable. In particular, we find that if the remnant black hole has a nonzero charge, the dilaton field persists. If it has both nonzero charge and spin, the axion field also persists after merger. These results suggest that Kerr--Sen black holes are stable under the conditions and time frames we considered.  

A natural follow-up study would be the analysis of waveforms produced by inspiraling black holes, an area where the available results remain relatively limited. In this paper, we have further assumed that we are working within the low-energy sector of string theory. A more detailed investigation of the theory space parameterized by $\alpha_0$ could be intriguing; in particular, the coupling $\alpha_0 = \sqrt{3}$ corresponds to the Kaluza--Klein theory \cite{Horne1992}. Future work may explore interpolations between these theories.
