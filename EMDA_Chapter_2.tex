\section{Introduction}
Gravitational-wave detectors have opened a new regime for precision tests of general relativity (GR)~\cite{Abbott2025GWTC3GR,Yunes2025GWTests,Abac2025GW250114,
Giri2025AdSBlackShells,Clough2024,Bambi2025Mimickers,
Yunes2013_LRR_GRTests,Yagi2016_BHTestsGR,Meidam2014,Berti2015,Yang2017}. Recent detections have reached signal-to-noise ratios as high as 80, enabling tests of Hawking's area law and placing constraints on the remnant spin at the 90 percent confidence level~\cite{LVK2025GW250114}. As detector sensitivity continues to improve, gravitational waves will provide increasingly precise probes of the strong-field, dynamical regime of gravity.

Although GR has passed all observational tests to date, it is not the only theoretically viable description of gravity. This motivates the study of well-posed alternative theories and their predictions for compact-binary dynamics. A broad range of approaches to strong-field gravity beyond GR has been explored in recent years~\cite{Cardoso2012NRHEP,Corman2024NonlinearModificationsGR,Afshordi2024BlackHolesInsideOut,Bambi2025BlackHoleMimickers,Bernard2025GenericEFTGWTests}. These include studies of dynamical Chern--Simons gravity~\cite{Alexander2009,Okounkova2019}, Einstein-dilaton-Gauss-Bonnet theory~\cite{Okounkova:2020rqw,Okounkova2020}, modified gauge choices in Einstein-scalar-Gauss-Bonnet theory~\cite{Ripley2020a,Ripley2020b}, the so-called ``fixing the equations'' approach~\cite{Cayuso2020}, and auxiliary-field formulations for higher-curvature systems, including quadratic gravity~\cite{Held2021,Held2023,Held2025,East2023,Taillte2025}. See also Ref.~\cite{Corman2024} for a more detailed discussion and comparison of these and related approaches.

There are, however, significant challenges associated with making concrete predictions in alternative theories of gravity, especially in the strong-field and highly dynamical regime relevant for compact-binary mergers. One particularly useful strategy is to construct waveform models in these theories and determine where, and to what extent, they depart from their GR counterparts. Such comparisons are essential for assessing whether beyond-GR effects could be observable with current or future gravitational-wave detectors.

Numerical relativity, and the waveform models derived from it, are essential for this effort because compact-binary mergers are highly nonlinear and analytic approximations are not accurate across large portions of the parameter space. Numerical studies of alternative theories require well-posed initial data, robust evolution equations, controlled constraint propagation, and stable gauge dynamics. These ingredients are prerequisites for extracting physically meaningful information from simulations and for constructing reliable waveform catalogs.

There has been significant work investigating binary black-hole waveforms in Einstein--Maxwell gravity~\cite{Bozzola2019,Zilhao2012,Zilhao2014,Bozzola2021GW150914ChargedBH,Bozzola2023ExtremalChargedMergers,Luna2022ChargedBBHKicks}. These studies have explored how electromagnetic charge modifies the merger dynamics, radiation, and recoil of binary black holes. In one such analysis, the GW150914 signal was found to be compatible with charge-to-mass ratios as large as $(Q/M \sim 0.3)$ in the binary components~\cite{Bozzola2021GW150914ChargedBH}. However, comparatively less work has extended these studies to charged binary black holes in beyond-GR theories of gravity.

In this work, we use Einstein--Maxwell--dilaton--axion (EMDA) theory as a testing ground for possible departures from GR. EMDA is a good candidate because the theory allows for a well posed initial data problem. EMDA extends Einstein--Maxwell theory by coupling the electromagnetic field to additional scalar and pseudoscalar degrees of freedom. It also arises as a low-energy limit of heterotic string theory and admits rotating charged black-hole solutions, such as the Kerr--Sen spacetime. These black holes differ from Kerr black holes while remaining observationally viable in some regimes. Recent work has investigated the stability of black holes and black-hole hair in EMDA theory~\cite{PopeRohrerWhiting2025,Carroll2026KerrSenStability}, and black-hole shadow studies using Event Horizon Telescope observations have not ruled out Kerr--Sen black holes~\cite{Sahoo2024PRD,Sahoo2025}.

These considerations motivate a systematic study of binary black-hole dynamics in EMDA theory. In particular, waveform catalogs for alternative theories of gravity remain limited, and comparatively little work has been done on inspiraling binary black-hole evolutions in EMDA theory. To our knowledge, the present work is among the first studies of inspiraling binary black-hole waveforms in this theory.

The cases considered here include charge-to-mass ratios larger than those expected for astrophysical black holes~\cite{Gibbons1975VacuumPolarization,Pan2019BlackHoleDischarge}. Our goal is therefore not to construct immediately realistic astrophysical waveform templates, but rather to probe where departures from GR become significant. This allows us to identify regimes in which the additional EMDA fields produce measurable changes in the binary dynamics and gravitational-wave signal.

\subsection{Motivation}

In this work, we present a modest catalog of binary black-hole waveforms in EMDA theory, considering several choices of mass ratio, spin, and charge configuration. The parameter space of compact-binary waveforms is highly degenerate. Consequently, it is not always clear whether deviations from a GR waveform arise from genuinely new physics or whether they could instead be mimicked by changes in intrinsic binary parameters, such as the mass ratio, spins, charges, or total mass scale.

A complete study of these degeneracies would require a full parameter-estimation analysis over both the EMDA and GR parameter spaces, which is beyond the scope of this work. Instead, we address a more restricted question: how do waveform differences emerge as one moves through the EMDA theory space, and under what conditions do these differences become appreciable?

\subsection{Goal of the Work}

The primary goal of this work is to determine if and under what circumstances  EMDA waveforms differ noticeably from their GR counterparts. We focus on how the additional EMDA fields and charges modify the inspiral dynamics and gravitational-wave signal. 

The waveform catalog presented here is intended as an initial step toward a more complete study of EMDA binary black-hole phenomenology. While we do not attempt to cover the full parameter space or perform a complete parameter-estimation analysis, these results provide useful guidance for future studies aimed at determining whether high-precision gravitational-wave observations can constrain or detect signatures of EMDA theory.

\section{Einstein--Maxwell--Dilaton--Axion}
\label{sec:emda}
The general theory we consider in this work is an extension of Einstein-Maxwell on inclusion of two coupled scalar fields, the dilaton and the axion.  The action for the EMDA system is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
S=\int \mathrm{d}^4 x\,\sqrt{-g}\Big[
&{R\over8\pi}-2(\nabla\phi)^2-e^{-2\alpha_0\phi}F^2\\
&-\tfrac12 e^{4\alpha_1\phi}(\nabla\kappa)^2
-\kappa\,F_{ab}(*F)^{ab}
\Big],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the first term is the standard Hilbert action and \(\phi\) and \(\kappa\) are the dilaton and axion, respectively.
\(F_{ab}\) is the U(1) field strength, and \((*F)_{ab}\) is
its dual. The parameters $\alpha_0$ and $\alpha_1$ are real, positive constants
that set the coupling strengths and allow us to consider a family of EMDA
theories. Particular choices correspond to particular theories. For
instance, $\alpha_0=\alpha_1=0$ gives Einstein--Maxwell theory minimally
coupled to scalar and axion fields, while
$(\alpha_0,\alpha_1)=(\sqrt{3},0)$ corresponds to Kaluza--Klein.
In this work we take $(\alpha_0,\alpha_1)=(1,1)$, corresponding to a
low-energy limit of heterotic string theory. For these coupling values, the
theory admits the Kerr--Sen family of rotating, charged black hole
solutions. These solutions reduce to Kerr when the electromagnetic charge
vanishes. In contrast to the Kerr--Newman solution of Einstein--Maxwell
theory, the Kerr--Sen geometry is accompanied by nontrivial dilaton and
axion fields whose structure is determined by the black hole charge and
spin. The explicit Kerr--Sen metric and associated fields are summarized in
Appendix~\ref{sec:appx:KerrSenBH}.



\section{Numerical methods}
\label{sec:methods}

To study the strong-field dynamics and gravitational radiation from inspiraling charged black-hole binaries in Einstein--Maxwell--Dilaton--Axion (EMDA) theory, we evolve the full EMDA system as a Cauchy initial-value problem. The spacetime metric is evolved using the BSSN formulation~\cite{Shibata1995_BSSN,Baumgarte1999_BSSN,Baumgarte2010}, together with puncture gauge conditions~\cite{Campanelli2006,Baker2006}. The electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion fields are evolved using the corresponding matter evolution equations obtained from the $3+1$ decomposition of the EMDA equations of motion. The full evolution system is summarized in Appendix~\ref{sec:appx:eom}.

We use the same code developed in earlier work~\cite{Carroll2026KerrSenStability}. In contrast to the head-on collisions considered there, the simulations presented here describe binary black holes with nonzero orbital angular momentum. The initial data are chosen to approximate inspiraling binary configurations, with the black holes assigned initial linear momenta appropriate for quasi-circular motion. This allows us to follow the late inspiral, merger, and ringdown, and to extract gravitational, electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion radiation from the resulting spacetime.

\subsection{Code Description}
\label{sec:code_description}

We perform our black hole merger simulations using \texttt{Dendro-GR}, a well developed numerical relativity framework. \texttt{Dendro-GR} combines an octree-based adaptive
mesh infrastructure with automatic code generation for the evolution
equations~\cite{Fernando2019,Fernando2019b,Fernando2023}. The computational domain is refined dynamically using wavelet adaptive multiresolution (WAMR) in which the evolved fields are represented on an adaptive hierarchy of octree blocks. Wavelet coefficients computed from the evolved variables provide a local estimate of the truncation error 
or unresolved field content~\cite{DeBuhr2018RelativisticHydroWavelets,Anderson2006RelativisticMHDAMR, Fernando2023}.
Regions where these wavelet coefficients exceed a prescribed tolerance are refined, while regions in which wavelet coefficients remain below a coarsening threshold are de-refined. This tends to concentrate resolution near the black holes, in the wave extraction region, and in regions where the electromagnetic, dilaton, or axion fields develop large spatial gradients, while smoother regions are evolved on coarser grids.

Spatial derivatives are computed using sixth-order finite-difference
stencils, and time integration is performed using a fourth-order
Runge--Kutta scheme. We include Kreiss--Oliger dissipation to damp
poorly resolved high-frequency modes and improve numerical stability
\cite{KreissOliger1973}. Additional details of the \texttt{Dendro-GR}
infrastructure, including its WAMR implementation, scalability, and previous applications to binary black hole evolutions, can be 
found in~\cite{Fernando2019,Fernando2019b,Fernando2023,Black2025_ORBIT}.

During the evolutions, we monitor the Hamiltonian and momentum constraints together with the electromagnetic divergence constraints throughout the computational domain. These quantities provide a global check on the consistency of the evolution and are used to assess the efficacy of the WAMR refinement parameters. Representative plots of the constraints and their violation are shown in Appendix~\ref{sec:code_performance}.

We also compute quasilocal quantities on apparent horizons using the
\texttt{BHaHAHA} apparent horizon finder~\cite{Etienne2026BHaHAHA}, which
we have extended to evaluate the horizon mass, spin, and electric charge.

\subsection{Controlling the Eccentricity}

In this work, we focus on approximately quasicircular inspiral orbits. For uncharged binary black holes, post-Newtonian methods can be used to set the initial momenta for low-eccentricity inspirals. In the EMDA system, however, the presence of electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion fields complicates the construction of low-eccentricity initial data. Post-Newtonian treatments have been developed for Einstein--Maxwell theory and for several scalar-modified theories of gravity~\cite{Khalil2018_EMD_EOB,Yagi2012_QuadraticGravityPN,Julie2019_EsGB_PN,Gupta2025_EinsteinMaxwell2PN}. In this work, we adopt a simpler prescription based on the charged-binary argument used in Ref.~\cite{Bozzola2021}.

At Newtonian order, both the gravitational and electromagnetic interactions are central forces. For two charged compact objects, the Coulomb interaction modifies the effective strength of the attractive force. Denoting the charge-to-mass ratio of each black hole by
\[
    \lambda_i = \frac{Q_i}{M_i},
\]
the leading-order effect of the electromagnetic interaction can be incorporated through the replacement
\[
    G \rightarrow \tilde{G} = G\left(1-\lambda_1\lambda_2\right).
\]
In units where \(G=1\), this becomes
\[
    \tilde{G} = 1-\lambda_1\lambda_2 .
\]
Thus, like-signed charges reduce the effective attraction, while oppositely signed charges increase it. We therefore first compute the momenta required for an uncharged binary to be approximately quasicircular and then rescale the orbital momenta according to the effective gravitational coupling implied by the charge-to-mass ratios of the two black holes.

The initial momenta for the corresponding uncharged binaries are computed using the \texttt{NRPyPN} package~\cite{Etienne2023_NRPyPN}. This package provides post-Newtonian estimates for low-eccentricity binary black-hole initial data compatible with Bowen--York/TwoPunctures data~\cite{Healy2017_PNQuasicircular,RamosBuades2019_EccentricityReduction}. We then apply the charge-dependent rescaling described above to obtain the momenta used in the charged evolutions. This procedure assumes that the leading correction to the orbital parameters comes from the electromagnetic interaction, while the direct effects of the dilaton and axion fields are negligible.

Although approximate, this prescription yields sufficiently low eccentricities for the configurations considered here, as illustrated in Figs.~\ref{fig:orbital_dynamics_q1_q2} and~\ref{fig:orbital_dynamics_q4_q8}.

We estimate the orbital eccentricity using an estimator based on the orbital frequency~\cite{RamosBuades2019_EccRed},
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_{\Omega}(t)
    =
    \frac{1}{2}
    \left[
    \frac{\Omega(t)-\Omega_{e=0}(t)}
         {\Omega_{e=0}(t)}
    \right],
    \label{eq:eccentricity_omega_estimator}
\end{equation}
where \(\Omega(t)\) is the measured orbital frequency and \(\Omega_{e=0}(t)\) denotes the corresponding non-eccentric reference trend.



An estimator based on the orbital frequency is used since it depends less on the choice of gauge than other quantities which can be used to estimate the eccentricity, such as the coordinate separation. We track the coordinate positions of each black hole during the inspiral simulation, compute the coordinate separation, and then compute the orbital frequency. This orbital frequency is then fit using a \verb|NonlinearModelFit| in \verb|Mathematica| based on post-Newtonian theory~\cite{RamosBuades2019_EccRed}.

When fitting the orbital frequency data, it is ideal to fit as many orbits as possible to reduce the error in the eccentricity estimate. However, the gauge settling at the beginning of the simulation provides numerical artifacts which are not physical, and the orbital frequency rises rapidly as the binary system's separation approaches their innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO) and the black holes begin to plunge. Thus, we often exclude the first $\sim 200 M$ of the simulation and set the end of the fitting window to where the coordinate separation is close to the ISCO (typically a separation of $\sim 6M$).

The measured orbital eccentricities for each catalog run are shown in Table \ref{tab:eccentricity_catalog}. The eccentricities are extrapolated from the fit to a time $t = 0M$ in the simulation. Note that each of the runs begin at a coordinate separation of $r = 10M$, $r = 9M$, and $r = 8M$ for $q = 1$, $q = 2$, and $q = 4$ or $q = 8$, respectively. The eccentricities are mostly all on the order of $10^{-3}$. As expected, the eccentricity is lower (i.e., the orbit is closer to quasi-circularity) for lower mass ratio, spin, and charge.

Figure \ref{fig:bh_eccentricity} shows an example of the orbital eccentricity data and post-Newtonian fit for one of the catalog runs. The data is fit for about 4 orbits and begins with a comparatively large eccentricity on the order of $10^{-2}$ which can be attributed to the larger separation, larger spin magnitude, larger charge magnitude, and repulsive charge configuration. The error in the eccentricity estimate is about 2.34\%, and the eccentricity decays over time due to the emission of gravitational radiation.

In practice, more quasi-circular orbits can be obtained by means of eccentricity reduction procedures in which the initial momenta are corrected by post-Newtonian theory after the eccentricity fit and the inspiral simulation is run again. For the sake of time, we do not reduce the eccentricity of these runs. Others (refs) have seen reductions of up to 10 times in the eccentricity. Standard post-Newtonian methods to measure and correct the eccentricity may fail to account for the electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion fields, and so we believe that it would take more iterations to reach a similar reduction in these runs. It may also be possible to include corrective terms for the EMDA fields in the eccentricity reduction analysis.

\subsection{Waveform Mismatch and Distinguishability}

Following Refs.~\cite{Damour1998_ImprovedFilters,Harry2011_TargetedCoherentSearch,Abbott2017_WaveformSystematicsGW150914,Bozzola2021GW150914ChargedBH,Bozzola2021}, we use waveform overlaps and mismatches to quantify differences between our simulated gravitational-wave signals.

A direct comparison between EMDA and GR waveforms does not, by itself, isolate the contribution of the scalar fields. In particular, part of the measured difference may arise from the electromagnetic charge rather than from the dilaton or axion fields. A more complete study would compare EMDA waveforms not only with vacuum GR, but also with Einstein--Maxwell evolutions having the same charge configurations. Such a comparison is beyond the scope of the present work. Our goal here is instead to demonstrate that charged EMDA binaries can produce waveform differences relative to GR that are measurable in principle.

To quantify the difference between two gravitational-wave signals, we compute the mismatch
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{M}(h_1,h_2) = 1 - \max \mathcal{O}(h_1,h_2),
\end{equation}
where the maximum is taken over relative time shifts and polarization angles. The overlap \(\mathcal{O}\) is defined by
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{O}(h_1,h_2) =
    \frac{(h_1,h_2)}
    {\sqrt{(h_1,h_1)(h_2,h_2)}} ,
\end{equation}
where \((h_1,h_2)\) denotes the noise-weighted inner product between the two waveforms. We evaluate this quantity over the full frequency range considered in the analysis.

This comparison does not include additional degeneracies associated with shifts in the intrinsic binary parameters, such as the masses, spins, or eccentricity. These degeneracies could partially absorb the effect of charge and would likely make charge-induced deviations more difficult to identify in a full parameter-estimation study. The purpose of the present comparison is therefore not to claim a unique observational signature of EMDA charge, but rather to demonstrate that the simulations provide a framework for estimating potentially detectable deviations from GR.

As a rough estimate of distinguishability, we associate a mismatch \(\mathcal{M}\) with the critical signal-to-noise ratio
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\rm crit} \simeq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\mathcal{M}}}.
\end{equation}
Thus, two waveforms with mismatch \(\mathcal{M}\) are expected to become distinguishable only for events with signal-to-noise ratios of order \(\rho_{\rm crit}\) or larger. This estimate should be interpreted cautiously, since it does not account for correlations with the full set of binary parameters.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We compare EMDA and GR waveforms for a representative set of binary black-hole configurations. In particular, we examine how the waveform differences depend on charge, mass ratio, and spin.

The simulation catalog considered in this work is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:simulation_catalog}. For each mass ratio \(q=m_1/m_2\), spin \(\chi\), and charge magnitude \(\lambda\), we consider the neutral case, \(Q_1=Q_2=0\), together with several charged configurations. The charged cases are denoted by \(++\), \(+-\), and \(+0\), corresponding respectively to like-signed charges, oppositely signed charges, and configurations in which only one black hole is charged.

We find that the waveform differences generally increase with both charge magnitude and spin. In each case, the charged waveform is compared against a neutral reference waveform with the same mass ratio and spin. For example, for the \(q=1\), \(\chi=0.4\) cases, the mismatch increases from \(2.5959\times 10^{-4}\) for \(\lambda=0.1\) to \(5.2775\times 10^{-3}\) for \(\lambda=0.3\). Similar trends are observed across the spinning cases. The full set of waveform mismatches is given in Table~\ref{tab:waveform_mismatches_combined}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this work, we have presented a small waveform catalog for binary black-hole simulations in Einstein--Maxwell--dilaton--axion (EMDA) theory. Our results show that, as the charge parameter is increased, the differences between the neutral general-relativistic reference waveforms and the charged EMDA waveforms become increasingly pronounced. This indicates that charge and the additional EMDA fields can leave measurable imprints on the gravitational waveform.

At the same time, these differences should be interpreted with care. Some of the observed deviations may be partially degenerate with changes in the binary mass ratio, spins, or other intrinsic parameters. In addition, not all differences between the charged and neutral cases can be attributed uniquely to the dilaton and axion sectors, since part of the effect may arise from the electromagnetic charge itself. A more complete comparison would therefore include corresponding Einstein--Maxwell simulations, allowing the purely electromagnetic contribution to be separated from the additional EMDA-field effects. Such a study is computationally expensive and is left for future work.

These results demonstrate that EMDA theory provides a useful numerical relativity testbed for studying beyond-GR effects in binary black-hole mergers. Even within this limited catalog, the simulations show that charged extensions of general relativity can produce waveform differences that grow systematically with charge, motivating further studies with larger parameter surveys and more detailed comparisons to both GR and Einstein--Maxwell binaries.
