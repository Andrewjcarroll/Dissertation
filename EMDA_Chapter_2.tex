\section{Introduction}

Gravitational-wave observations now provide direct access to the strong-field, dynamical regime of compact-binary coalescence. As detector sensitivities improve and high signal-to-noise-ratio detections become more likely, the late inspiral, merger, and ringdown will offer increasingly stringent tests of the general-relativistic description of black holes \cite{Abbott2025GWTC3GR,Yunes2025GWTests,Yagi2016_BHTestsGR,Berti2015,Abac2025GW250114}. Recent detections have reached signal-to-noise ratios as high as $80$, enabling tests of Hawking's area law and improved measurements of the remnant black hole \cite{LVK2025GW250114}. These developments make it increasingly important to understand not only whether observed signals are consistent with general relativity, but also how specific beyond-GR effects would appear in the waveform.

A central difficulty in such tests is that the merger regime is both highly nonlinear and theory dependent. For many alternative theories of gravity, analytic approximations are insufficient near merger, while numerical simulations require a well-posed initial-value formulation, stable gauge choices, and controlled constraint behavior. Considerable effort has therefore been devoted to developing formulations and approximation schemes that permit nonlinear simulations beyond GR \cite{Cardoso2012NRHEP,Corman2024NonlinearModificationsGR,Afshordi2024BlackHolesInsideOut,Bambi2025Mimickers,Bernard2025GenericEFTGWTests}. Examples include studies of dynamical Chern--Simons gravity \cite{Alexander2009,Okounkova2019}, Einstein-dilaton-Gauss-Bonnet theory \cite{Okounkova:2020rqw,Okounkova2020}, modified gauge choices in Einstein-scalar-Gauss-Bonnet theory \cite{Ripley2020a,Ripley2020b}, the ``fixing the equations'' approach \cite{Cayuso2020}, and auxiliary-field formulations for higher-curvature systems, including quadratic gravity \cite{Held2021,Held2023,Held2025,East2023,Taillte2025}. See also Ref.~\cite{Corman2024} for a more detailed comparison of these approaches.

In this work, we focus on the waveform-level consequences of Einstein--Maxwell--dilaton--axion (EMDA) theory. EMDA extends Einstein--Maxwell theory by coupling the electromagnetic field to a dilaton and an axion, and it arises as a low-energy limit of heterotic string theory \cite{Sen1992,Sen1995}. The theory admits rotating charged black-hole solutions, such as the Kerr--Sen spacetime, whose exterior fields differ from those of Kerr--Newman black holes. Previous work has investigated the stability of black holes and black-hole hair in EMDA theory \cite{PopeRohrerWhiting2025,Carroll2026KerrSenStability}, and black-hole shadow studies using Event Horizon Telescope observations have not ruled out Kerr--Sen black holes \cite{Sahoo2024PRD,Sahoo2025}. These features make EMDA a useful setting in which to study how additional matter fields can influence compact-binary dynamics and gravitational radiation.

Charged binary black holes have also been studied extensively in Einstein--Maxwell gravity \cite{Bozzola2019,Zilhao2012,Zilhao2014,Bozzola2021GW150914ChargedBH,Bozzola2023ExtremalChargedMergers,Luna2022ChargedBBHKicks}. These simulations show that electromagnetic charge can modify the orbital dynamics, emitted radiation, and recoil of the remnant. In one analysis, the GW150914 signal was found to be compatible with charge-to-mass ratios as large as $Q/M\sim0.3$ \cite{Bozzola2021GW150914ChargedBH}. Comparatively little work, however, has examined charged inspiraling binary black holes in beyond-GR theories with additional scalar or pseudoscalar degrees of freedom.

The goal of this paper is to take a step toward such a waveform catalog in EMDA theory. We simulate inspiraling charged binary black holes and compare the resulting gravitational waveforms with neutral general-relativistic reference waveforms. The cases considered here include charge-to-mass ratios larger than those expected for astrophysical black holes \cite{Gibbons1975VacuumPolarization,Pan2019BlackHoleDischarge}. Our aim is therefore not to construct immediately realistic astrophysical templates, but to identify regimes in which the additional EMDA fields produce measurable changes in the binary dynamics and gravitational-wave signal. In this sense, the simulations provide a controlled probe of how charge, dilaton, and axion effects can enter gravitational waveforms in the nonlinear merger regime.

We present a modest catalog of binary black-hole waveforms in EMDA theory, considering several choices of mass ratio, spin, and charge configuration. Our primary goal is to determine when EMDA waveforms begin to differ appreciably from their neutral GR counterparts and to identify how these differences depend on the charge configuration and binary parameters.

An important complication is that compact-binary waveforms are highly degenerate. Differences between an EMDA waveform and a GR waveform need not imply an unambiguous beyond-GR signature, since similar changes could potentially be mimicked by varying intrinsic binary parameters such as the mass ratio, spins, total mass scale, or, in charged systems, the charge. A complete assessment of these degeneracies would require parameter estimation over both the EMDA and GR parameter spaces, which is beyond the scope of this study.

Instead, we address a more restricted question: how do waveform differences emerge as one moves through the EMDA parameter space, and under what conditions do these differences become large enough to detect? The catalog presented here is intended as an initial step toward a more complete study of EMDA binary black-hole phenomenology and toward assessing whether high-precision gravitational-wave observations could constrain or detect signatures of EMDA theory.

\section{Einstein--Maxwell--dilaton--axion Theory}
\label{sec:emda}

We consider Einstein--Maxwell--dilaton--axion (EMDA) theory, in which the Einstein--Maxwell system is supplemented by a scalar dilaton field $\phi$ and a pseudoscalar axion field $\kappa$. The action is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
S = \int \mathrm{d}^4 x,\sqrt{-g}\Big[
&\frac{R}{8\pi}
-2\nabla_a\phi\nabla^a\phi
-e^{-2\alpha_0\phi}F_{ab}F^{ab} \
&-\frac{1}{2}e^{4\alpha_1\phi}\nabla_a\kappa\nabla^a\kappa
-\kappa F_{ab}({}^{*}F)^{ab}
\Big],
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:emda_action}
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the Ricci scalar, $F_{ab}$ is the $U(1)$ field strength, and $({}^{*}F)_{ab}$ is its dual. The constants $\alpha_0$ and $\alpha_1$ determine the coupling strengths between the electromagnetic field and the dilaton and axion sectors.

Different choices of $\alpha_0$ and $\alpha_1$ correspond to different members of this class of theories. For example, $\alpha_0=\alpha_1=0$ gives Einstein--Maxwell theory together with minimally coupled scalar and pseudoscalar fields, while $(\alpha_0,\alpha_1)=(\sqrt{3},0)$ gives the Kaluza--Klein value of the dilaton coupling \cite{Horne1992,Mignemi1993_ChargedStringBH}. In the simulations presented here, we fix
\begin{equation}
(\alpha_0,\alpha_1)=(1,1),
\end{equation}
which corresponds to the coupling values associated with the low-energy limit of heterotic string theory \cite{Sen1992,Sen1995}.

For this choice of couplings, the theory admits rotating charged black-hole solutions of the Kerr--Sen type \cite{Sen1992}. These solutions reduce to Kerr in the limit of vanishing electromagnetic charge, but for nonzero charge the black hole is accompanied by nontrivial dilaton and axion fields. This makes EMDA a useful extension of Einstein--Maxwell theory for studying how charge can affect binary black-hole dynamics when additional matter fields are present.

The inspiraling binaries studied in this work are not exact binary Kerr--Sen solutions. Instead, we construct approximate charged binary black-hole initial data and evolve the full EMDA system numerically. This allows us to compare the resulting gravitational waveforms against neutral general-relativistic reference waveforms and to identify how the electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion fields influence the inspiral and merger. The isolated Kerr--Sen solution and associated fields are summarized in Appendix~\ref{sec:appx:KerrSenBH}.

\section{Numerical methods}
\label{sec:methods}

To study the strong-field dynamics and gravitational radiation from inspiraling charged black-hole binaries in Einstein--Maxwell--dilaton--axion (EMDA) theory, we evolve the full EMDA system as a Cauchy initial-value problem. The spacetime metric is evolved using the BSSN formulation \cite{Shibata1995_BSSN,Baumgarte1999_BSSN,Baumgarte2010}, together with puncture gauge conditions \cite{Campanelli2006,Baker2006}. The electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion fields are evolved using the corresponding matter evolution equations obtained from the $3+1$ decomposition of the EMDA equations of motion. The full evolution system is summarized in Appendix~\ref{sec:appx:eom}.

We use the code developed in earlier work \cite{Carroll2026KerrSenStability}. In contrast to the head-on collisions considered there, the simulations presented here describe binary black holes with nonzero orbital angular momentum. The initial data are chosen to approximate inspiraling binary configurations, with the black holes assigned initial linear momenta appropriate for quasicircular motion. This allows us to follow the late inspiral, merger, and ringdown, and to extract gravitational, electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion radiation from the resulting spacetime.

\subsection{Code description}
\label{sec:code_description}

For the simulations presented here, we evolve the EMDA system using \texttt{Dendro-GR}, the numerical relativity code also used in Ref.~\cite{Carroll2026KerrSenStability}. \texttt{Dendro-GR} is an adaptive mesh-refinement code based on an octree representation of the computational domain. The mesh hierarchy is adjusted dynamically using wavelet adaptive multiresolution, in which wavelet coefficients provide a local measure of unresolved field content and are used to determine where refinement or coarsening is required \cite{DeBuhr2018RelativisticHydroWavelets,Anderson2006RelativisticMHDAMR,Fernando2023}. This allows resolution to be concentrated in regions where the evolved fields contain sharp gradients or otherwise require finer spatial resolution. In the simulations considered here, this places additional resolution near the black holes and in regions with nontrivial electromagnetic, dilaton, or axion structure, while avoiding uniform refinement of the full computational domain.

Spatial derivatives are evaluated using sixth-order finite-difference operators, and the evolution is advanced in time with a fourth-order Runge--Kutta method. We also add Kreiss--Oliger dissipation of the corresponding order to suppress poorly resolved high-frequency numerical modes. Further details on the \texttt{Dendro-GR} infrastructure, including its adaptive wavelet refinement strategy, code-generation framework, scalability, and previous applications to binary black-hole simulations, can be found in Refs.~\cite{Fernando2019,Fernando2019b,Fernando2023,Black2025_ORBIT}. In particular, \texttt{Dendro-GR} has been shown to perform well for intermediate-mass-ratio inspiral simulations, where adaptive refinement is especially useful for resolving disparate length scales \cite{Fernando2019b,Black2025_ORBIT}.

Throughout each evolution, we monitor the Hamiltonian and momentum constraints, as well as the electromagnetic divergence constraints. These quantities are used to track the consistency of the numerical solution and to assess whether the chosen refinement parameters adequately resolve the relevant dynamics. Representative constraint behavior is shown in Appendix~\ref{sec:code_performance}.

We also extract quasilocal horizon quantities using a modified version of the \texttt{BHaHAHA} apparent-horizon finder \cite{Etienne2026BHaHAHA}. In addition to locating the apparent horizons, this modified version is used to compute the horizon mass, spin, and electric charge during the evolution.

\subsection{Controlling the Eccentricity}

In this work, we focus on approximately quasicircular inspiral orbits. For uncharged binary black holes, post-Newtonian methods can be used to set the initial momenta for low-eccentricity inspirals. In the EMDA system, however, the presence of electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion fields complicates the construction of low-eccentricity initial data. Post-Newtonian treatments have been developed for Einstein--Maxwell theory and for several scalar-modified theories of gravity~\cite{Khalil2018_EMD_EOB,Yagi2012_QuadraticGravityPN,Julie2019_EsGB_PN,Gupta2025_EinsteinMaxwell2PN}. In this work, we adopt a simpler prescription based on the charged-binary argument used in Ref.~\cite{Bozzola2021}.

At Newtonian order, both the gravitational and electromagnetic interactions are central forces. For two charged compact objects, the Coulomb interaction modifies the effective strength of the attractive force. Denoting the charge-to-mass ratio of each black hole by
\[
    \lambda_i = \frac{Q_i}{M_i},
\]
the leading-order effect of the electromagnetic interaction can be incorporated through the replacement
\[
    G \rightarrow \tilde{G} = G\left(1-\lambda_1\lambda_2\right).
\]
In units where \(G=1\), this becomes
\[
    \tilde{G} = 1-\lambda_1\lambda_2 .
\]
Thus, like-signed charges reduce the effective attraction, while oppositely signed charges increase it. We therefore first compute the momenta required for an uncharged binary to be approximately quasicircular and then rescale the orbital momenta according to the effective gravitational coupling implied by the charge-to-mass ratios of the two black holes.

The initial momenta for the corresponding uncharged binaries are computed using the \texttt{NRPyPN} package~\cite{Etienne2023_NRPyPN}. This package provides post-Newtonian estimates for low-eccentricity binary black-hole initial data compatible with Bowen--York/TwoPunctures data~\cite{Healy2017_PNQuasicircular,RamosBuades2019_EccRed}. We then apply the charge-dependent rescaling described above to obtain the momenta used in the charged evolutions. This procedure assumes that the leading correction to the orbital parameters comes from the electromagnetic interaction, while the direct effects of the dilaton and axion fields are negligible.

Although approximate, this prescription yields sufficiently low eccentricities for the configurations considered here, as illustrated in Figs.~\ref{fig:orbital_dynamics_q1_q2} and~\ref{fig:orbital_dynamics_q4_q8}.We also note that the charge has a signifiicant effect on the amount of time it takes to inspirial in.

We estimate the orbital eccentricity using an estimator based on the orbital frequency~\cite{RamosBuades2019_EccRed},
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_{\Omega}(t)
    =
    \frac{1}{2}
    \left[
    \frac{\Omega(t)-\Omega_{e=0}(t)}
         {\Omega_{e=0}(t)}
    \right],
    \label{eq:eccentricity_omega_estimator}
\end{equation}
where \(\Omega(t)\) is the measured orbital frequency and \(\Omega_{e=0}(t)\) denotes the corresponding non-eccentric reference trend.



An estimator based on the orbital frequency is used since it depends less on the choice of gauge than other quantities which can be used to estimate the eccentricity, such as the coordinate separation. We track the coordinate positions of each black hole during the inspiral simulation, compute the coordinate separation, and then compute the orbital frequency. This orbital frequency is then fit using a \verb|NonlinearModelFit| in \verb|Mathematica| based on post-Newtonian theory~\cite{RamosBuades2019_EccRed}.

When fitting the orbital frequency data, it is ideal to fit as many orbits as possible to reduce the error in the eccentricity estimate. However, the gauge settling at the beginning of the simulation provides numerical artifacts which are not physical, and the orbital frequency rises rapidly as the binary system's separation approaches their innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO) and the black holes begin to plunge. Thus, we often exclude the first $\sim 200 M$ of the simulation and set the end of the fitting window to where the coordinate separation is close to the ISCO (typically a separation of $\sim 6M$).

The measured orbital eccentricities for each catalog run are shown in Table \ref{tab:eccentricity_catalog}. The eccentricities are extrapolated from the fit to a time $t = 0M$ in the simulation. Note that each of the runs begin at a coordinate separation of $r = 10M$, $r = 9M$, and $r = 8M$ for $q = 1$, $q = 2$, and $q = 4$ or $q = 8$, respectively. The eccentricities are mostly all on the order of $10^{-3}$. As expected, the eccentricity is lower (i.e., the orbit is closer to quasi-circularity) for lower mass ratio, spin, and charge.

Figure \ref{fig:bh_eccentricity} shows an example of the orbital eccentricity data and post-Newtonian fit for one of the catalog runs. The data is fit for about 4 orbits and begins with a comparatively large eccentricity on the order of $10^{-2}$ which can be attributed to the larger separation, larger spin magnitude, larger charge magnitude, and repulsive charge configuration. The error in the eccentricity estimate is about 2.34\%, and the eccentricity decays over time due to the emission of gravitational radiation.

In practice, more quasi-circular orbits can be obtained by means of eccentricity reduction procedures in which the initial momenta are corrected by post-Newtonian theory after the eccentricity fit and the inspiral simulation is run again. For the sake of time, we do not reduce the eccentricity of these runs. Others (refs) have seen reductions of up to 10 times in the eccentricity. Standard post-Newtonian methods to measure and correct the eccentricity may fail to account for the electromagnetic, dilaton, and axion fields, and so we believe that it would take more iterations to reach a similar reduction in these runs. It may also be possible to include corrective terms for the EMDA fields in the eccentricity reduction analysis.

\subsection{Waveform mismatch and distinguishability}

Following Refs.~\cite{Damour1998_ImprovedFilters,Harry2011_TargetedCoherentSearch,Abbott2017_WaveformSystematicsGW150914,Bozzola2021GW150914ChargedBH,Bozzola2021}, we use waveform overlaps and mismatches to quantify differences between our simulated gravitational-wave signals.

A direct comparison between EMDA and GR waveforms does not, by itself, isolate the contribution of the scalar fields. In particular, part of the measured difference may arise from the electromagnetic charge rather than from the dilaton or axion fields. A more complete study would compare EMDA waveforms not only with vacuum GR, but also with Einstein--Maxwell evolutions having the same charge configurations. Such a comparison is beyond the scope of the present work. Our goal here is instead to demonstrate that charged EMDA binaries can produce waveform differences relative to GR that are measurable in principle.

To quantify the difference between two gravitational-wave signals, we compute the mismatch
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}(h_1,h_2) = 1 - \max \mathcal{O}(h_1,h_2),
\end{equation}
where the maximum is taken over relative time shifts and polarization angles. The overlap $\mathcal{O}$ is defined by
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}(h_1,h_2) =
\frac{(h_1,h_2)}
{\sqrt{(h_1,h_1)(h_2,h_2)}} ,
\end{equation}
where $(h_1,h_2)$ denotes the noise-weighted inner product between the two waveforms. In practice, we evaluate this quantity over the frequency range used in the analysis.

This comparison does not include additional degeneracies associated with shifts in the intrinsic binary parameters, such as the masses, spins, or eccentricity. These degeneracies could partially absorb the effect of charge and would likely make charge-induced deviations more difficult to identify in a full parameter-estimation study. The purpose of the present comparison is therefore not to claim a unique observational signature of EMDA charge, but rather to demonstrate that the simulations provide a framework for estimating potentially detectable deviations from GR.

As a rough estimate of distinguishability, we associate a mismatch $\mathcal{M}$ with the critical signal-to-noise ratio
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\rm crit} \simeq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\mathcal{M}}}.
\end{equation}
Thus, two waveforms with mismatch $\mathcal{M}$ are expected to become distinguishable only for events with signal-to-noise ratios of order $\rho_{\rm crit}$ or larger. This estimate should be interpreted cautiously, since it does not account for correlations with the full set of binary parameters.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We compare EMDA and GR waveforms for a representative set of binary black-hole configurations. In particular, we examine how the waveform differences depend on charge, mass ratio, and spin.

The simulation catalog considered in this work is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:simulation_catalog}. For each mass ratio \(q=m_1/m_2\), spin \(\chi\), and charge magnitude \(\lambda\), we consider the neutral case, \(Q_1=Q_2=0\), together with several charged configurations. The charged cases are denoted by \(++\), \(+-\), and \(+0\), corresponding respectively to like-signed charges, oppositely signed charges, and configurations in which only one black hole is charged.

We find that the waveform differences generally increase with charge. In each case, the charged waveform is compared against a neutral reference waveform with the same mass ratio and spin. For example, for the $(q=1)$, $(\chi=0.4)$ cases, the mismatch increases from $(2.5959\times 10^{-4})$ for $(\lambda=0.1)$ to $(5.2775\times 10^{-3})$ for $(\lambda=0.3)$. The full set of waveform mismatches is analyzed using the numerical-relativity analysis toolkit kuibit~\cite{kuibit}, and the results are given in Table~\ref{tab:waveform_mismatches_combined}. The critical signal-to-noise ratio required to distinguish the charged and neutral waveforms ranges from approximately $(2.3)$ to $(537)$.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this work, we presented a small waveform catalog for binary black-hole simulations in Einstein--Maxwell--dilaton--axion (EMDA) theory. Our results show that, as the charge parameter is increased, the differences between the neutral general-relativistic reference waveforms and the charged EMDA waveforms become increasingly pronounced. This indicates that electric charge and the additional EMDA fields can leave observable imprints on the gravitational waveform. Using the mismatch as a rough distinguishability estimate, the largest deviations in our catalog correspond to critical signal-to-noise ratios of order a few, with the smallest value approximately $(\rho_{\rm crit}\simeq 2.15)$. These values suggest that the largest waveform differences found here could be distinguishable in sufficiently loud events, although a full parameter-estimation study would be required to make this statement quantitative.

These differences should be interpreted with care. Some of the observed deviations may be partially degenerate with changes in the binary mass ratio, spins, eccentricity, or other intrinsic parameters. In addition, not all differences between the charged and neutral cases can be attributed uniquely to the dilaton and axion fields, since part of the effect may arise from the electromagnetic charge itself. A more complete comparison would therefore include corresponding Einstein--Maxwell simulations, allowing the purely electromagnetic contribution to be separated from the additional EMDA-field effects. Such a study is computationally expensive and is left for future work.

These results demonstrate that EMDA theory provides a useful numerical-relativity testbed for studying beyond-GR effects in binary black-hole mergers. Even within this limited catalog, the simulations show that charged extensions of general relativity can produce waveform differences that grow systematically with charge. This motivates further studies with larger parameter surveys, improved initial data, longer inspirals, and detailed comparisons among neutral GR, Einstein--Maxwell, and EMDA binaries.
